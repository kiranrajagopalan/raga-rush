# Raga Rush — Why I Built This

**Format:** Talking head + simple graphics + app footage
**Duration:** ~3 minutes
**Tone:** Conversational, personal, warm

---

## Act 1: The Seed
**~45 seconds**

`[VISUAL: Talking head. Warm, close framing. Soft light.]`

My dad used to take me to Karnatik music concerts when I was a kid. I didn't fully understand what was happening, but something about it pulled me in.

In Karnatik music, before a singer performs a song, they do something called an alaapana. It's an improvisation — a preview of what's coming. Think of it as an ad-lib. They're not singing the song yet. They're giving you a taste of its palette.

And that's when the guessing game starts.

My dad would lean over and whisper, "Does this sound familiar?" And I'd sit there with my tiny ears, trying really hard to find the patterns. Because every raga has certain phrases that identify it — and the musician reveals them slowly, teasing the audience.

`[VISUAL: Optional — subtle overlay of concert hall / cassette tapes, warm tones]`

There are levels to this game. Can you name the raga? Can you name it before the person sitting next to you? And the hardest — can you guess what composition they're about to sing?

That was the most intriguing part of going to concerts. And looking back, it was the first pattern-matching exercise I ever did.

## Act 2: The Color Palette
**~40 seconds**

`[VISUAL: Transition to simple graphics — color palettes forming on screen. Kiran's voice over.]`

Here's how I think about it. Imagine you're painting a scenery, but you're only allowed seven colors. For the entire painting, you have to stick to those seven. That palette — that constraint — is the raga.

Some paintings use five colors. Some use seven. And within those, your red could be a brick red or a crimson. Subtle variations that change everything.

`[VISUAL: Two side-by-side palettes — similar but different shades. Then palettes dissolving into new ones.]`

Now, before you start painting, you show the audience your palette. That's the alaapana. And if you've seen enough paintings, you start recognizing — "I know this palette. I've seen it before."

That recognition? That's raga identification. That's the game.

## Act 3: The Rush
**~40 seconds**

`[VISUAL: Back to talking head. More energy, leaning forward.]`

Later, I discovered this format called Ragam Tanam Pallavi. The singer isn't performing someone else's composition — they become the composer. They write and perform their own piece, live.

And towards the end, something incredible happens. They start switching ragas. Rapidly. One raga for ten seconds, then another, then another.

Like a monkey jumping between branches.

`[VISUAL: Quick flash of the app's slot machine spinning — just a glimpse]`

You're trying to identify each one before they leap to the next. It's this beautiful challenge — identify what's happening, don't expect what's coming, in a very short window of time.

That rush — that rapid context-switching — is what fascinated me.

---

## Act 4: The Connection
**~30 seconds**

`[VISUAL: Talking head. Reflective pace.]`

I realized much later that this was my first pattern-matching exercise. Long before I became a designer.

We do pattern matching all the time in design. You see a typeface and try to identify the family, find the closest match, understand its DNA. It's the same muscle. Ragas trained it first.

`[VISUAL: Optional — brief split-screen: a typeface specimen on one side, a raga name on the other. Same instinct.]`

I was always fascinated by tricking my brain into switching contexts fast. Like tasting things blindfolded in rapid succession — sweet, then something unknown, then reset. Your brain scrambling to adapt. I wanted to build something that exercises that muscle.

---

## Act 5: The App + The Why
**~45 seconds**

`[VISUAL: Quick cuts of the app — slot machine spinning, singing on camera, countdown ring ticking, video generating, share sheet opening. ~8 seconds total, energetic.]`

So I built Raga Rush. It's a roulette of ragas. You don't know what's coming. You have seconds to sing it, and then you move on. The whole thing is recorded as a video you can share.

`[VISUAL: Back to talking head. Slower, grounded.]`

It recreates that feeling from the concert hall — the monkey jumping branches — but now it's a practice tool. And a game.

There's a quote I think about often. Bong Joon-ho, the director of Parasite, once quoted Martin Scorsese: "The most personal is the most creative."

`[VISUAL: Slight pause. Direct to camera.]`

This is the first of hopefully many attempts at something I believe in — not solving problems, but exploring opportunities I love, in fields I love, and building things for people like me.

`[VISUAL: Hold on Kiran's face for a beat. Cut to black. Karnatricks wordmark.]`
